Is My Child Ready for School? 10 Signs to Look For

By NurtureNest Editors
Reviewed by Lisa Fernandez, Early Education Expert
4 min read

School readiness isn't just about knowing the alphabet. It's about a whole mix of social, emotional, physical, and cognitive skills that help your child thrive in a classroom. Here's what to look for — and what really matters most.

👩‍🏫
Expert Insight — Lisa Fernandez, Early Education Specialist

"The number one thing I look for isn't academic knowledge — it's whether a child can manage their emotions and follow simple instructions. Everything else can be taught." More at NAEYC — School Readiness.

The 10 Signs Your Child Is Ready

  • 1
    Can follow 2–3 step instructions — "Put on your shoes, get your bag, and wait at the door."
  • 2
    Can manage basic self-care — using the toilet independently, washing hands, opening their lunchbox.
  • 3
    Can separate from you without extreme distress — some nerves are normal, but they should recover within a few minutes.
  • 4
    Shows curiosity and asks questions — "Why?" and "How?" are great signs of a ready learner.
  • 5
    Can sit and focus for 10–15 minutes — on a story, puzzle, or activity they enjoy.
  • 6
    Can take turns and share — not perfectly, but with some understanding of fairness.
  • 7
    Uses language to communicate needs — can ask for help, express feelings, and make basic requests.
  • 8
    Recognises their own name in writing — and shows interest in letters, numbers, or books.
  • 9
    Can hold a pencil or crayon — fine motor skills for writing develop through drawing and play.
  • 10
    Shows excitement about learning — enthusiasm and confidence matter more than academic knowledge at this stage.
💡 Important Reminder

Every child develops at their own pace. Missing a few of these signs doesn't mean your child isn't ready — it means they may need a little more time or support. Talk to your child's doctor or a preschool teacher if you have concerns.

What Doesn't Matter as Much as You Think

Many parents worry about whether their child can count to 100, read words, or write full sentences before starting school. While these are lovely achievements, they're not what teachers need most from incoming students.

What matters far more is emotional readiness — can they manage frustration, wait their turn, and ask for help? Children who enter school with strong social-emotional skills consistently outperform those who only have academic preparation, according to research from Harvard's Center on the Developing Child.

How to build readiness at home

The best school prep happens naturally through play, conversation, and routine. Read together every day. Let them help with simple chores. Play board games that require turn-taking. Give them choices and let them experience small disappointments — it builds resilience.
