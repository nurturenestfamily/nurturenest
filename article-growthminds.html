Building a Growth Mindset in Your Child

By NurtureNest Editors
Reviewed by Dr. James Okafor, Child Psychologist
6 min read

The single most powerful belief a child can hold about themselves is this: "I can get better at things with effort." This is what psychologist Carol Dweck calls a growth mindset — and it predicts success in school, relationships, and life far more reliably than IQ or natural talent.

🧠
Expert Insight — Dr. James Okafor, Child Psychologist

"The way we praise children shapes their relationship with effort and failure. 'You're so smart' creates fragility. 'You worked so hard' builds resilience." Research at Harvard — Resilience & Growth Mindset.

Fixed vs Growth Mindset

A fixed mindset believes ability is innate and unchangeable — "I'm either good at maths or I'm not." A growth mindset believes ability develops through effort and learning — "I'm not good at maths yet, but I can improve." Children with growth mindsets embrace challenges, persist through difficulty, and recover from failure more quickly.

How Parents Accidentally Create Fixed Mindsets

  • Praising intelligence: "You're so clever" → creates fear of tasks where they might not look clever
  • Protecting from failure: swooping in before they struggle → removes the learning opportunity
  • Catastrophising mistakes: "Oh no, you got that wrong" → signals that errors are shameful
  • Comparing to other children: breeds competition rather than personal growth

How to Build a Growth Mindset

Praise effort, strategy, and process

"I love how you kept trying even when it was hard." "I noticed you tried a different approach when the first one didn't work." "That mistake showed you where you need more practice — that's actually really useful."

Normalise struggle

"Learning something new always feels hard at first. That feeling of struggle is your brain growing." Share your own learning challenges: "I found this really difficult when I first tried it too."

Celebrate "not yet"

When a child says "I can't do this," add "yet." It's a small word with a powerful implication: this is a current state, not a permanent one.

💡 The Power of Yet

Teach your child to add "yet" to any "I can't" statement. "I can't ride a bike" becomes "I can't ride a bike yet." This tiny linguistic shift opens the door to possibility and signals that growth is always available.
