Teaching Children About Money: Age-by-Age Financial Guide

By NurtureNest Editors
Reviewed by Marcus & Tina Webb, Parent Coaches
6 min read

Financial literacy is one of the most important life skills we can give our children — yet it's rarely taught in schools. Parents who talk openly about money, involve children in financial decisions, and teach saving and giving from an early age raise children who are confident, responsible adults with money.

👨‍👧‍👦
Expert Insight — Marcus & Tina Webb, Parent Coaches

"The families who raise financially confident children are the ones who talk about money openly — not to stress children out, but to normalise it as a tool that can be understood and managed." Research at CFPB — Youth Financial Education.

Why Financial Education Starts at Home

Research from Cambridge University found that children's money habits are largely formed by age 7. The conversations — and silences — around money in your home are already shaping your child's financial future. The good news: it's never too early or too late to start.

Age-by-Age Money Lessons

Ages 3–5: Introducing the concept

  • Show that money is exchanged for things — involve them at the checkout
  • A simple piggy bank introduces saving
  • Teach: "We can't buy everything we want right now"

Ages 6–9: Earning and saving

  • Introduce a small allowance for age-appropriate chores
  • The three-jar system: Spend, Save, Give — divide money between all three
  • Set a savings goal for something they want — and let them experience the patience required
  • Involve them in the supermarket — compare prices, talk about choices

Ages 10–13: Budgeting basics

  • Give a larger monthly allowance and have them manage their own clothing budget
  • Introduce the concept of needs vs wants
  • Talk about where the family's money comes from and where it goes (age-appropriate)
  • Introduce the concept of interest and saving in a real bank account

Teenagers: Real world preparation

  • Encourage part-time work — earning builds respect for money differently than receiving it
  • Teach how bank accounts, debit cards, and credit work
  • Discuss university costs, student loans, and long-term financial planning
  • Involve them in family financial decisions where appropriate
💡 Talk About Money Openly

The biggest predictor of children's financial wellbeing is how openly their family discussed money growing up. You don't need to share your salary — but normalise conversations about prices, choices, savings goals, and generosity. Money shouldn't be a taboo.
