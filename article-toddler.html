Toddler Tantrums: Why They Happen & How to Respond Calmly

By NurtureNest Editors
Reviewed by Dr. James Okafor, Child Psychologist
5 min read

Your toddler was perfectly happy one minute — and now they're on the floor, screaming, because you cut their toast the wrong way. Sound familiar? Tantrums are one of the most challenging parts of toddlerhood, but understanding why they happen makes all the difference.

🧠
Expert Insight — Dr. James Okafor, Child Psychologist

"Tantrums aren't bad behaviour — they're a developmental sign that your child's emotional brain is outpacing their ability to regulate it. Your calm response literally helps wire their brain for better self-control." Read more at the Zero to Three Foundation.

Why Tantrums Happen

Toddlers between ages 1–3 are experiencing a huge emotional awakening. They have big feelings — frustration, disappointment, hunger, tiredness — but almost no ability to regulate or express them in words yet. The result? A meltdown.

The science behind it

The prefrontal cortex — the part of the brain responsible for emotional regulation — doesn't fully develop until the mid-20s. Toddlers are quite literally neurologically incapable of "just calming down." They need you to be their calm.

Common tantrum triggers

  • Hunger or tiredness (the two biggest ones)
  • Transitions — moving from one activity to another
  • Being told "no" or having something taken away
  • Overstimulation — too much noise, activity, or screen time
  • Not being understood — wanting to communicate but lacking the words

How to Respond in the Moment

✅ What Helps

  • Stay calm and lower your voice
  • Get down to their level physically
  • Acknowledge the feeling: "You're really upset"
  • Offer a hug if they want one
  • Wait it out — they will calm down
  • Talk about it after, when calm

❌ What Doesn't Help

  • Shouting or matching their energy
  • Giving in to stop the crying
  • Threatening or shaming
  • Trying to reason during the meltdown
  • Ignoring them completely
  • Making them feel bad afterward
💡 The "Sportscaster" Technique

Narrate what you see without judgment: "You really wanted that cookie and I said no. That made you feel so frustrated." This validates their feeling without giving in — and builds emotional vocabulary over time.

Preventing Tantrums Before They Start

You can't prevent every tantrum — but you can reduce them significantly with a few simple habits.

  • Keep consistent nap and meal times — a tired or hungry toddler is a ticking clock
  • Give transition warnings: "Five more minutes, then bath time"
  • Offer limited choices: "Do you want the red cup or the blue cup?"
  • Notice and name emotions throughout the day, not just during meltdowns
  • Build in enough downtime — overscheduled toddlers melt down more
