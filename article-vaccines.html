Childhood Vaccines: What Every Parent Needs to Know

By NurtureNest Editors
Reviewed by Dr. Sarah Mitchell, Pediatrician
7 min read

Few topics in parenting generate more questions than vaccines. As a parent, you want to understand what your child is receiving, why, and what to expect. Here is a clear, evidence-based overview from a paediatric perspective — answering the questions parents ask most often.

👩‍⚕️
Expert Insight — Dr. Sarah Mitchell, Pediatrician

"Vaccines are one of the greatest public health achievements in human history. They have eliminated or dramatically reduced diseases that once killed or disabled millions of children every year. The science on vaccine safety is among the most studied in medicine." Full schedule at CDC — Childhood Vaccine Schedule.

Why Do Children Need Vaccines?

Vaccines protect children from serious, potentially life-threatening diseases by training the immune system to recognise and fight specific pathogens. They also protect communities — particularly newborns and immunocompromised individuals who cannot be vaccinated — through herd immunity. Vaccination is both a personal and a community act of care.

The Recommended Vaccine Schedule

The CDC and American Academy of Pediatrics recommend a carefully timed vaccine schedule designed to protect children during their most vulnerable periods. Vaccines are given when they're needed most — not on a convenience schedule. The timing is based on extensive research into when children are most susceptible to specific diseases and when their immune systems respond most effectively.

Common Parental Questions

Are multiple vaccines at once safe?

Yes. Research consistently shows that receiving multiple vaccines at one visit is safe and does not overwhelm the immune system. Babies encounter thousands of antigens daily in their environment — the immune load from vaccines is negligible by comparison. Combining vaccines also reduces the number of office visits and earlier protection.

What side effects are normal?

Mild soreness, redness, or swelling at the injection site, low-grade fever, and fussiness for 1–2 days are common and normal. They indicate the immune system is responding. Serious reactions are extremely rare — your child's healthcare provider will discuss signs to watch for.

What about the MMR vaccine and autism?

The claim that the MMR vaccine causes autism originated from a 1998 study that was subsequently found to be fraudulent and was retracted by its journal. Dozens of large-scale studies involving millions of children across multiple countries have found no link between any vaccine and autism. The AAP, WHO, and CDC all confirm vaccine safety.

💡 Comfort Tips for Vaccine Day

Breastfeed or offer a bottle during or immediately after injections — sucking reduces pain response. For older children, bring a comfort object, use distraction (tablet, favourite song), and stay calm yourself — children read parental anxiety. After-visit treats make the experience more positive.
